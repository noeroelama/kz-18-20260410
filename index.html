Chapter 1 — The Problem

The Current Model

In LSTM-GSTARX, the spatial influence term computed at every time step is:

\[ \mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_{\text{static}} \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^* \]

What is F*?

A weighted average of rainfall from neighboring provinces — the "spatial lag" that enters the LSTM input alongside past rainfall and climate indices.

What is W_static?

An \(N \times N\) matrix computed once from inverse Haversine distances between province centroids. Computed before training, never updated.

The Core Issue
\(\mathbf{W}_{\text{static}}\) assumes that the spatial influence between any two provinces is constant forever — regardless of season, ENSO phase, or IOD.
So let me show you where we start. The existing LSTM-GSTARX model computes a spatial lag term — F-star — at every time step by multiplying a static weight matrix with last month's rainfall vector. The key word here is STATIC. W-static is computed once before training from the geographic distances between province centroids, using inverse Haversine weighting. It encodes the assumption: the closer two provinces are, the more they influence each other. And this never changes. The spatial lag F-star is then combined with past rainfall and the climate indices — Niño 3.4 and DMI — as input to the LSTM. So the model does receive climate information, but it uses that information only for prediction. The spatial aggregation step itself is completely blind to climate conditions. Let me show you why this is physically wrong.
Chapter 1 — The Problem

A Physically Wrong Assumption

Normal conditions

  • Walker Circulation active
  • Maritime Continent receives convection
  • Neighboring provinces share monsoon patterns
  • → Geographic proximity ≈ spatial influence ✓

El Niño conditions

  • Walker Circulation weakens
  • Convection shifts east toward Pacific
  • Indonesia-wide drought — but heterogeneous
  • → Geographic proximity ≠ spatial influence ✗
Observed during El Niño 1997–98 and 2015–16
West Java, Sulawesi, and NTT: severe drought. Papua: relatively wet. Two geographically distant regions become negatively correlated — but a static weight matrix treats them as weakly positive all year round.
Here is the physical argument. Under normal monsoon conditions, provinces that are geographically close share similar rainfall patterns — they are in the same monsoon zone, affected by the same local convection. So inverse distance weights make good sense. But when El Niño occurs, the Walker Circulation weakens. The rising branch of convection that normally sits over Indonesia shifts eastward toward the central Pacific. The result: western and central Indonesia experience drought. But Papua and parts of eastern Indonesia remain wetter than normal — because they are partially protected from this mechanism. So during the 1997-98 and 2015-16 El Niño events, the rainfall anomaly between West Java and Papua was actually negative — they moved in opposite directions. Geographic proximity cannot tell you this. Only the climate state can. This is the physical justification for why W must change with time.
Chapter 1 — The Problem

Three Mechanisms That Break Static Weights

  1. ENSO (Niño 3.4)
    El Niño weakens Walker Circulation → convection center shifts east → drought in western/central Indonesia, wet anomaly in Papua. Teleconnections bypass geographic proximity. (Feng et al., 2010)
  2. IOD (DMI)
    Positive IOD cools SST off Sumatra → reduces evaporation and convection → drier western Indonesia (Sumatra, Java), less effect on eastern regions. Independent of ENSO, but often co-occurs. (Saji et al., 1999)
  3. Monsoon transitions
    West monsoon (Nov–Mar) vs east monsoon (May–Sep) redirect moisture flow, reversing dominant direction of spatial influence between island groups.
Consequence
The spatial weight between any two provinces is a function of time — specifically, a function of the current climate state. We need \(\mathbf{W}_t\), not \(\mathbf{W}_{\text{static}}\).
More specifically, there are three mechanisms that cause spatial dependencies to change over time. First, ENSO — which I just described. The Walker Circulation shifts and distant provinces become correlated through large-scale teleconnections rather than local geography. Second, the IOD — Indian Ocean Dipole. A positive IOD event cools SST off Sumatra, reducing local evaporation and convection. This preferentially affects western Indonesia — Sumatra, Java, Bali — while eastern regions like Papua and Maluku are much less affected. This happens independently of ENSO, though both often occur together. Third, monsoon transitions. During the west monsoon from November to March, moisture flows from the west — affecting western provinces first. During the east monsoon from May to September, the direction reverses. This seasonally changes the dominant direction of spatial influence. The conclusion is clear: we need a weight matrix that changes each month. Let me now show you what CASA does.

Requirements for \(\mathbf{W}_t\):

CASA delivers all five

Climate-Adaptive Spatial Attention produces \(\mathbf{W}_t\) through a three-stage mechanism that adds only 265 parameters — 0.72% of the total model.

So what do we need exactly? Five things. The weight matrix must update each month. It must know about ENSO and IOD. But crucially — it should not completely abandon geography. During normal, neutral months, geographic proximity is still a perfectly good predictor of spatial influence. So the ideal system uses geography as a default, and deviates from it when climate conditions warrant. Fourth, it must be parameter-efficient. With only 240 months of training data and 38 provinces, we cannot afford to add thousands of new parameters — that would overfit immediately. And fifth, we want formal guarantees — specifically, that CASA can never perform worse than the static model on training data. CASA delivers all five. Let me show you how.

The core idea

At each time step, CASA produces a fresh \(\mathbf{W}_t\) by blending two matrices:

  • \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\): static geographic prior (Haversine)
  • \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t\): dynamic attention, conditioned on climate state \(\mathbf{c}_t\)

The blend ratio \(\alpha_t\) is itself a function of climate — so geography dominates during normal months, and attention takes over during ENSO events.

Key design decision
Geography as regularization anchor: the model starts from a physically meaningful prior and learns when to deviate from it.
Here is the architecture overview. CASA has three stages. Stage 1 compresses the current climate state into a context vector c_t of dimension 16. This vector will condition everything that follows. Stage 2 runs in parallel: 2a computes a learned attention matrix over all province pairs, conditioned on c_t; 2b computes a scalar blending gate alpha_t, also conditioned on c_t. Stage 3 produces the final weight matrix by blending the geographic prior and the learned attention, with alpha_t controlling the ratio. The key design decision — which differentiates CASA from pure attention approaches — is that we keep the geographic prior as what I call a regularization anchor. The model has a physically meaningful starting point, and it only deviates from geography when the data and climate conditions justify it. Let me go through each stage in detail.

Mean and standard deviation of last month's rainfall capture the national-scale situation — is Indonesia as a whole dry or wet, and is the anomaly concentrated in specific regions or uniform across all provinces. And practically, adding 38 values would require W_c to be 16 × 42 instead of 16 × 4, adding about 1,500 parameters. With only 240 months of data, this would overfit severely. Chapter 2 — Architecture

Stage 2a: Attention Scores

For every pair \((i, j)\) — all \(38 \times 38 = 1{,}444\) pairs — we compute:

\[ e_{ij}^t = \mathbf{v}_a^\top \cdot \underbrace{\text{LeakyReLU}}_{\gamma=0.2}\!\left(\mathbf{W}_a \begin{bmatrix} Y_{t-1,i} \\ Y_{t-1,j} \\ |Y_{t-1,i} - Y_{t-1,j}| \\ \mathbf{c}_t \end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{b}_a\right) \]

Inputs to each pair:

  • \(Y_i\) — condition of the "asking" province
  • \(Y_j\) — condition of the "answering" province
  • \(|Y_i - Y_j|\) — how different they are right now
  • \(\mathbf{c}_t\) — current climate regime

Output: a scalar relevance score \(e_{ij}^t\)

→ Higher score = province \(j\) is more relevant to province \(i\) this month

Key: GATv2 ordering
LeakyReLU applied before projection → truly dynamic attention (explained in Ch. 3)
Stage 2a computes an attention score for every pair of provinces. The score answers the question: "this month, how relevant is province j for predicting province i?" The four inputs to each pair are: the rainfall of province i, the rainfall of province j, their absolute difference, and the climate context c_t from Stage 1. The structure is: concatenate these inputs, multiply by W_a, add bias b_a, apply LeakyReLU, then project to a scalar with v_a. The key design decision — which I will prove formally in Chapter 3 — is the ordering of operations. LeakyReLU is applied BEFORE the final projection v_a. This is the GATv2 pattern. The original GAT paper does it the other way: project first, then apply LeakyReLU. That ordering, I will show, produces attention that cannot change its ranking of neighbors regardless of which province is asking. GATv2 fixes this. Let me explain LeakyReLU quickly before moving on.
Chapter 2 — Architecture

LeakyReLU — The Activation Function

\[ \text{LeakyReLU}(x) = \begin{cases} x & x > 0 \\ \gamma x & x \leq 0 \end{cases}, \quad \frac{d}{dx} = \begin{cases} 1 & x > 0 \\ \gamma & x < 0 \end{cases} \]
Problem with plain ReLU (γ = 0)
If a neuron's input is consistently negative, gradient = 0 → weights never update → neuron "dies" and cannot recover.
LeakyReLU fix (γ = 0.2)
Gradient is always ≥ γ = 0.2 for all inputs. Neurons cannot die. All paths carry gradients during training.

Why γ = 0.2? Convention from GAT (Veličković et al., 2018). Too small → approaches ReLU; too large → loses nonlinearity. 0.2 balances both.

Red dashes = ReLU (dies for x<0)
Teal = LeakyReLU (always alive)

LeakyReLU is a simple modification of the standard ReLU activation. ReLU outputs zero for any negative input — which sounds fine, but it means the gradient through that neuron is also zero. If a neuron consistently receives negative inputs, it stops contributing to learning entirely. This is called the dying ReLU problem. LeakyReLU fixes this by keeping a small slope gamma for negative inputs. With gamma = 0.2 — which is the convention from the original GAT paper — negative inputs produce outputs that are 20% of their original magnitude. The gradient is always 0.2, so learning never stops. In our CASA attention MLP, this is important because the input vector includes the absolute difference |Yi - Yj|, which can be zero when two provinces have identical rainfall. Without LeakyReLU, those neurons could die. With it, gradients flow through all attention computations. Now, why LeakyReLU and not tanh or sigmoid? Because the attention score e_ij is used to compute a softmax — we want scores to span a wide range so softmax creates sharp, discriminative weights. Tanh and sigmoid saturate and give gradients near zero for large inputs, which slows training badly.
Chapter 2 — Architecture

Full \(N \times N\) Softmax

\[ \tilde{\alpha}_{ij}^t = \frac{\exp(e_{ij}^t)}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{N} \exp(e_{ik}^t)} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N} \text{ (row-stochastic)} \]

GAT uses restricted softmax:

\[\sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \exp(e_{ik})\] — only geographic neighbors
Provinces not in the edge list get zero weight permanently. The model cannot learn distant teleconnections, even during strong El Niño.

CASA uses full softmax:

\[\sum_{k=1}^{N} \exp(e_{ik})\] — all 38 provinces
Every pair is eligible. Model learns which connections matter from data — including long-range teleconnections.

Feasible because N=38: only 1,444 pairs per step. Geography still enters via \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\) in the blending. (Precedent: Graph WaveNet, Wu et al. 2019)

After computing the raw attention scores, we normalize them using softmax so they sum to 1 — making A_learned a row-stochastic matrix. The crucial difference from the original GAT is the scope of the softmax. GAT normalizes only over geographic neighbors — the edge list that someone manually defined. CASA normalizes over all 38 provinces. Why does this matter? Because ENSO teleconnections do not follow geographic adjacency. During a strong El Niño, Papua and Java can be anti-correlated — if you restrict attention to geographic neighbors, the model literally cannot represent this. The correct weight between them is permanently forced to zero. A potential concern: is full N×N attention too expensive? No. With N=38, we have 1,444 pairs — trivially small. Graph WaveNet used the same approach for traffic networks with 207 nodes. We are well within that regime. Geography still matters — it enters through A_geo in the blending step. So the model starts from geography and learns when to extend beyond it.

Stage 3: Final blend

\[\mathbf{W}_t = \alpha_t \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} + (1-\alpha_t) \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t\]

Adaptive spatial lag

\[\mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_t \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^*\]
Stage 2b computes a scalar gate alpha_t using a sigmoid function applied to a linear projection of the climate context vector. Alpha_t is a number between 0 and 1. Think of it as asking: "How much should I trust geography right now?" When alpha is close to 1, geography dominates. When alpha is close to 0, the learned attention takes over. The key is that alpha_t is DYNAMIC — it changes every month based on c_t, which encodes ENSO and IOD conditions. So during neutral months, alpha naturally stays high, and the model behaves almost like the static baseline. During strong El Niño events, alpha drops, and the model relies on what it has learned about teleconnections. Stage 3 then combines: W_t is a convex combination of A_geo and A_learned, with alpha_t controlling the ratio. And the spatial lag F-star is computed as before, just using W_t instead of W_static. Chapter 3 — Proofs

Proof A: GAT Produces Static Attention

🖊 Whiteboard — follow this derivation step by step

Setup. GAT (Veličković 2018) computes: \(e_{ij} = \mathbf{a}^\top \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i \| \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j)\)

Partition \(\mathbf{a} = [\mathbf{a}_L \| \mathbf{a}_R]\) corresponding to left and right halves.

(1) Since \(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i\) and \(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j\) are independent before concatenation: \[ \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i \| \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j) = [\text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i) \| \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j)] \]
(2) Therefore the score decomposes additively: \[ e_{ij}^{\text{GAT}} = \underbrace{\mathbf{a}_L^\top \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i)}_{g(i)} + \underbrace{\mathbf{a}_R^\top \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j)}_{h(j)} \]
(3) Comparing two keys \(j_1\) vs \(j_2\) for any query \(i\): \[ e_{ij_1} > e_{ij_2} \iff g(i)+h(j_1) > g(i)+h(j_2) \iff \class{accent}{h(j_1) > h(j_2)} \] This condition is independent of \(i\). So the ranking of any two keys never changes. \(\square\)
GATv2 fix
\(e_{ij}^{\text{GATv2}} = \mathbf{v}^\top \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}[\mathbf{h}_i \| \mathbf{h}_j])\) — \(\mathbf{W}\) sees both \(h_i\) and \(h_j\) together before nonlinearity → no additive decomposition → truly dynamic ✓
Let me go to the whiteboard and walk through this carefully. [AT WHITEBOARD] We start with the GAT formula. GAT applies the weight matrix W separately to h_i and h_j, concatenates them, then applies LeakyReLU. The key is that LeakyReLU is applied ELEMENT-WISE after concatenation. Step 1: because W*h_i and W*h_j are independent, and LeakyReLU is applied element-wise, the result decomposes into two independent parts — the left half depends only on h_i, the right half only on h_j. Step 2: when we compute the inner product with the attention vector a, we can split it as a_L times the left part plus a_R times the right part. Call these g(i) and h(j). Step 3: now ask — for a fixed query node i, which key node j scores higher, j_1 or j_2? The answer is: h(j_1) > h(j_2). Notice that g(i) cancels out! This comparison does not depend on i at all. So for any query node i-prime, the ranking of j_1 vs j_2 is the same. The attention is monotone — hence static. GATv2 fixes this by feeding the concatenation of h_i and h_j TOGETHER into W BEFORE the nonlinearity. Now W can learn cross-terms between the two. The additive decomposition breaks. Rankings can change depending on which province is asking. CASA uses the GATv2 ordering, and additionally adds c_t to the input, so climate state also influences how the ranking changes.

Proof B: \(\mathbf{W}_t\) is Row-Stochastic

Claim: \(\sum_j W_t(i,j) = 1\) and \(W_t(i,j) \geq 0\) for all \(i,j\).

(1) \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\) is row-stochastic by construction: entries are normalized inverse distances, so non-negative and rows sum to 1.
(2) \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t\) is row-stochastic: each row is computed via softmax, which always produces non-negative outputs summing to 1.
(3) Row sum of \(\mathbf{W}_t\): \[ \sum_j W_t(i,j) = \alpha_t \underbrace{\sum_j A_{\text{geo}}(i,j)}_{=\,1} + (1-\alpha_t)\underbrace{\sum_j \tilde{\alpha}_{ij}^t}_{=\,1} = \alpha_t + (1-\alpha_t) = 1 \quad \checkmark \]
(4) Non-negativity: \(\alpha_t \in (0,1)\) and all entries of both matrices are \(\geq 0\), so all entries of \(\mathbf{W}_t \geq 0\). \(\square\)
Interpretation
\(\mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_t \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^*\) is a convex combination (weighted average) of normalized rainfall values. It has a direct physical interpretation as "the average climate influence on province \(i\)."
Proof B establishes that W_t is row-stochastic — meaning each row is non-negative and sums to one. Step 1: A_geo is row-stochastic by construction. We compute each row's entries as inverse distances, then divide by the row sum to normalize. Step 2: A_learned is row-stochastic because each row is the output of softmax, which by definition gives non-negative values summing to 1. Step 3: The row sum of W_t equals alpha times 1 plus (1 minus alpha) times 1 — which is just 1. Clean. Step 4: Non-negativity follows because alpha is strictly between 0 and 1, so both coefficients are positive, and we are adding positive multiples of non-negative matrices. The interpretation is important: F-star is now a proper weighted average of rainfall values across provinces. It has a physical meaning — it represents the weighted climate influence received by province i from all other provinces. This makes the model's outputs interpretable.
Chapter 3 — Proofs

Proof C: CASA Subsumes the Static Model

Claim: If \(b_\alpha \to +\infty\), then \(\mathbf{W}_t = \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\) for all \(t\) — CASA reduces exactly to LSTM-GSTARX with static weights.

(1) \(\mathbf{c}_t = \tanh(\cdot) \in (-1,1)^{d_c}\) is bounded. So \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t\) is bounded for any fixed \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha\).
(2) As \(b_\alpha \to +\infty\): the argument \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t + b_\alpha \to +\infty\). Since \(\sigma(x) \to 1\) as \(x \to +\infty\), we get \(\alpha_t \to 1\) for all \(t\).
(3) \(\mathbf{W}_t = 1 \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} + 0 \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t = \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\). \(\square\)
Guarantee
The static model is in CASA's search space. Gradient descent cannot produce a CASA model with higher training loss than the static baseline. CASA is risk-free to try.
What optimizer does if no pattern found
Simply drives \(b_\alpha\) large → \(\alpha_t \to 1\) → effectively switches off the adaptive component. CASA gracefully degrades to the static model.
Proof C is perhaps the most practically important. It says: the static model is a special case of CASA. This gives us a formal guarantee that CASA cannot perform worse than the static baseline on training data. The proof is simple. The context vector c_t is bounded because tanh output is always between -1 and 1. Therefore w_alpha dot c_t is bounded by the norm of w_alpha. As we drive b_alpha to positive infinity, the sigmoid input also goes to infinity, and sigmoid saturates at 1. So alpha_t becomes 1 for all time steps, and W_t becomes exactly A_geo. The practical implication: if the data contains no useful adaptive patterns — if ENSO and IOD don't actually help improve spatial prediction — the optimizer will naturally drive b_alpha large, essentially switching off the adaptive component. We pay no cost for adding CASA to the model when it isn't needed. This is a strong guarantee for adding any new module.
Chapter 3 — Proofs

Proof D: Bounded Deviation from Geographic Prior

Claim: \(\|\mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \leq (1-\alpha_t)\|\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F\)

(1) Compute the difference directly: \[ \mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} = (1-\alpha_t)\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t + \alpha_t \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} = (1-\alpha_t)(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}) \]
(2) Take Frobenius norm, using \((1-\alpha_t) > 0\): \[ \|\mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F = (1-\alpha_t)\|\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \leq (1-\alpha_t)\|\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \quad \square \]
Implicit regularization
\(\alpha_t\) automatically controls how far \(\mathbf{W}_t\) can deviate from geography. No explicit regularization penalty needed.
Numerical example (Ch. 4)
At \(\alpha_t = 0.32\) (El Niño moderate): deviation is at most 68% of the max possible. At neutral (\(\alpha_t \approx 0.85\)): at most 15%.
Proof D is straightforward once you see the algebra. Step 1: subtract A_geo from both sides of the blending equation. The A_geo terms combine, leaving (1 minus alpha_t) times the difference between A_learned and A_geo. Step 2: take the Frobenius norm. Since (1 minus alpha_t) is a positive scalar, it factors out. The result is an equality, and the inequality is trivially satisfied. The interpretation is what makes this interesting. The blending gate alpha_t directly controls how far W_t can stray from the geographic prior. During neutral months when alpha is around 0.85, the deviation is at most 15% of the maximum possible — geography is tightly enforced. During strong El Niño when alpha drops to 0.2, the deviation can be up to 80% — but the model has explicitly decided, based on climate conditions, that this is appropriate. This is an implicit regularizer: by design, the model cannot deviate arbitrarily from the geographic prior. We get this for free without adding any explicit L2 penalty on the learned attention weights.
Chapter 3 — Proofs

What the Proofs Guarantee Together

Proof B — Well-defined computation
\(\mathbf{F}_{t-1}^*\) is always a valid weighted average. The model produces physically interpretable outputs at every time step.
Proof C — Safety guarantee
Training loss of CASA ≤ training loss of static model. Adding CASA cannot hurt. If no adaptive pattern exists, the model degrades gracefully to static weights.
Proof D — Regularization guarantee
The geographic prior acts as an anchor. Deviation is bounded by the blending gate — no runaway learned attention.
Proof A — Motivation
The original GAT cannot produce truly dynamic attention. GATv2 ordering is necessary. CASA inherits this fix and extends it with climate conditioning.
Let me summarize what these four proofs give us collectively. Proof B says the computation is well-defined: F-star is always a proper weighted average with a clear physical interpretation. Proof C is the safety net: we can try CASA without risk. The worst case is that it behaves identically to the static model — it cannot be worse on training data. This is a strong argument for adding the module. Proof D gives us regularization for free. The geographic prior limits how far the model can deviate from known physical structure. And Proof A motivates our specific design choice: we use the GATv2 ordering to ensure attention is genuinely dynamic.
