Chapter 1

The Problem: Why Static Spatial Weights Fail

1.1 Current Model

The existing LSTM-GSTARX model computes a spatial lag term at each time step as:

\[ \mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_{\text{static}} \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^* \]

where \(\mathbf{W}_{\text{static}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}\) is computed once from inverse Haversine distances between province centroids and frozen for the entire study period.

Core Assumption (Physically Wrong)
The spatial influence that province \(j\) exerts on province \(i\) is constant over time — regardless of whether Indonesia is experiencing a normal monsoon, a severe El Niño, or an extreme positive IOD event.

1.2 Why This Assumption Fails

Three climate mechanisms cause spatial dependencies to change over time:

  1. ENSO events weaken the Walker Circulation, shifting the ascending branch of convection away from the Maritime Continent. Provinces that normally co-vary due to shared monsoon patterns can become decoupled, while geographically distant provinces may become coherent through large-scale teleconnections.
  2. IOD events alter the SST gradient across the Indian Ocean, changing moisture transport toward western Indonesia. This differentially affects western provinces (Sumatra, Java) vs. eastern provinces (Papua, Maluku).
  3. Monsoon transitions (west vs. east monsoon) redirect moisture flow, reversing the direction of spatial influence between regions.
Physical Intuition
During a strong El Niño (e.g., 1997–98 or 2015–16), Papua tends to remain relatively wet while Java experiences severe drought. The spatial dependency between these two regions flips sign. A static weight matrix cannot represent this.

1.3 What We Need

We need a weight matrix \(\mathbf{W}_t\) that:

  1. Updates at every time step \(t\)
  2. Responds to ENSO and IOD conditions at time \(t\)
  3. Preserves geographic structure as a regularization anchor during normal conditions
  4. Adds minimal parameters (to avoid overfitting with \(T = 240\) monthly observations)

This is what CASA (Climate-Adaptive Spatial Attention) provides.

Chapter 2

CASA Architecture

2.1 Overview

CASA replaces the single static matrix \(\mathbf{W}_{\text{static}}\) with a three-stage mechanism that produces a fresh \(\mathbf{W}_t\) at each time step. The rest of the LSTM-GSTARX architecture is unchanged.

2.2 Stage 1 — Climate Context Vector

We first compress the current climate state into a low-dimensional summary vector \(\mathbf{c}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}\):

\[ \mathbf{c}_t = \tanh\!\left(\mathbf{W}_c \begin{bmatrix} \bar{Y}_{t-1} \\ \sigma_{Y_{t-1}} \\ \text{Niño3.4}_t \\ \text{DMI}_t \end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{b}_c\right) \]

where \(\mathbf{W}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c \times 4}\), \(\mathbf{b}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}\), \(d_c = 16\).

InputWhat it encodesWhy include it
\(\bar{Y}_{t-1}\)Mean rainfall across all provinces last monthIs Indonesia generally wet or dry right now? A nationally dry mean signals the model that spatial weight should perhaps shift away from geographic defaults.
\(\sigma_{Y_{t-1}}\)Standard deviation of rainfall across provincesIs the anomaly spatially concentrated or uniform? High σ means some provinces are very wet while others are very dry — exactly when geography may mislead and adaptive weights matter most.
Niño 3.4\(_t\)ENSO phase (Pacific SST anomaly)Walker Circulation strength — primary driver of large-scale spatial rainfall redistribution across Indonesia.
DMI\(_t\)Indian Ocean Dipole indexMoisture transport toward western Indonesia — independently modifies spatial patterns especially in Sumatra and Java.
Why Mean and Variance — not raw Y values?

We deliberately use summary statistics of the rainfall field rather than the full \(\mathbf{Y}_{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{38}\) vector for two reasons.

(1) Dimensionality. Including all 38 raw values in \(\mathbf{c}_t\) would make \(\mathbf{W}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c \times 42}\) instead of \(\mathbb{R}^{d_c \times 4}\), adding ~1,500 parameters just for the context encoder — overwhelming the 265-parameter CASA budget and risking overfitting with only \(T = 240\) months of training data.

(2) Physical sufficiency. What matters for determining the climate regime at time \(t\) is not "how much did West Java rain?" (that is a local prediction signal), but rather: "Is the whole country dry or wet? Is rainfall spatially heterogeneous or uniform?" Mean and standard deviation capture exactly these two regime-defining quantities. The full spatial detail is handled separately by the per-pair attention scores \(e_{ij}^t\) in Stage 2, which do receive individual \(Y_i\) and \(Y_j\) values.

2.3 Stage 2a — Dynamic Attention Scores

For every pair of provinces \((i, j)\), we compute a relevance score at time \(t\):

\[ e_{ij}^t = \mathbf{v}_a^\top \cdot \text{LeakyReLU}\!\left(\mathbf{W}_a \begin{bmatrix} Y_{t-1,i} \\ Y_{t-1,j} \\ |Y_{t-1,i} - Y_{t-1,j}| \\ \mathbf{c}_t \end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{b}_a\right) \]

where \(\mathbf{W}_a \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a \times (3+d_c)}\), \(\mathbf{v}_a \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a}\), \(d_a = 8\), and LeakyReLU uses negative slope \(\gamma = 0.2\).

Design Choice: GATv2 Ordering
The operand order — LeakyReLU applied before the final linear projection \(\mathbf{v}_a^\top\) — follows GATv2 (Brody et al., 2022). This makes the attention truly dynamic: the ranking of province \(j\)'s relevance to province \(i\) depends on the specific combination of \(i\), \(j\), and \(\mathbf{c}_t\). The original GAT ordering (Veličković et al., 2018) produces static rankings — see Proof A in Chapter 3.

LeakyReLU — The Nonlinearity Inside the Attention MLP

Before the attention score is projected to a scalar by \(\mathbf{v}_a^\top\), the intermediate vector passes through a LeakyReLU nonlinearity. Understanding what this does — and why it is used instead of alternatives — is important for following the gradient flow during training.

Step 1: What is ReLU?

The Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) is the simplest nonlinearity widely used in deep learning:

\[ \text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x) = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x > 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x \leq 0 \end{cases} \]

ReLU is fast to compute and avoids the vanishing gradient problem that plagued older activations like sigmoid and tanh (which saturate — their gradients approach zero for large inputs). For positive inputs, ReLU's gradient is exactly 1, so gradients flow without shrinking.

Step 2: The "Dying ReLU" Problem

The Problem with Plain ReLU

ReLU outputs exactly zero for any negative input, and its gradient is also zero there. If a neuron's input happens to be consistently negative during training, the gradient flowing through it becomes zero — the neuron stops learning entirely. It is said to have "died."

Once dead, the neuron cannot recover, because with zero gradient the weights never update. In a network with many neurons, some fraction can die and remain permanently inactive, reducing the model's effective capacity.

Step 3: LeakyReLU — The Fix

LeakyReLU keeps a small nonzero slope \(\gamma\) for negative inputs (Maas et al., 2013):

\[ \text{LeakyReLU}(x) = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x > 0 \\ \gamma x & \text{if } x \leq 0 \end{cases} \]

The gradient is now:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\text{LeakyReLU}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x > 0 \\ \gamma & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases} \]

The gradient is never zero — even for negative inputs, it equals \(\gamma\). Neurons cannot die.

Step 4: Numerical Example

Suppose an intermediate attention vector \(\mathbf{z} = [-0.5, \; 1.2, \; -0.1, \; 0.8]\) passes through LeakyReLU with \(\gamma = 0.2\):

Negative values are kept — scaled down to 20% of their original magnitude — rather than being killed. All gradients are nonzero, so backpropagation can update every weight.

Step 5: Why \(\gamma = 0.2\) Specifically?

Convention from GAT (Veličković et al., 2018)

The slope \(\gamma = 0.2\) was chosen by the original GAT paper and has become a convention in graph attention architectures. It represents a balance:

  • Too small (\(\gamma \approx 0\)): approaches plain ReLU, dying neuron risk returns
  • Too large (\(\gamma \approx 1\)): approaches a linear function, the nonlinearity is too weak to separate complex patterns
  • \(\gamma = 0.2\): negative inputs contribute 20% of their magnitude — enough to keep gradients alive, small enough to still distinguish positive from negative activations meaningfully

In CASA, we follow this same convention to align with GAT/GATv2 literature and ensure fair comparison.

Why Not Use tanh or Sigmoid Here?

The attention MLP is a score function, not a probability output. We want scores \(e_{ij}^t\) to span a wide real-valued range so that softmax can create meaningful contrasts between pairs. Both sigmoid (output ∈ (0,1)) and tanh (output ∈ (−1, 1)) saturate — their gradients approach zero for large inputs, making training slow. LeakyReLU is unbounded above, so scores can grow freely and softmax normalization creates sharp, discriminative attention weights.

Reference: Maas, A. L., Hannun, A. Y., & Ng, A. Y. (2013). "Rectifier Nonlinearities Improve Neural Network Acoustic Models." ICML Workshop on Deep Learning for Audio, Speech, and Language Processing. — Original LeakyReLU proposal. The slope 0.2 convention is from Veličković et al. (2018), GAT.

Scores are normalized via full-scope softmax over all \(N\) provinces:

\[ \tilde{\alpha}_{ij}^t = \frac{\exp(e_{ij}^t)}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{N} \exp(e_{ik}^t)} \]

This produces \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}\) — a row-stochastic matrix of learned spatial attention weights.

2.4 Stage 2b — Climate-Conditioned Blending Gate

\[ \alpha_t = \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t + b_\alpha\right) \in (0, 1) \]

The gate \(\alpha_t\) is a scalar that answers: "How much should the model trust geography right now?"

Climate conditionExpected \(\alpha_t\)Interpretation
Normal / neutral ENSO≈ 0.7 – 0.9Geography dominates; spatial patterns are as expected
Moderate El Niño (Niño 3.4 ≈ 1.2)≈ 0.3 – 0.5Learned patterns become significant
Strong El Niño (Niño 3.4 ≥ 1.8)≈ 0.1 – 0.3Teleconnection patterns dominate over geography

2.5 Stage 3 — Adaptive Weight Matrix

\[ \mathbf{W}_t = \alpha_t \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} + (1-\alpha_t) \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t \]

The final spatial lag passed to the LSTM is then:

\[ \mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_t \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^* \]

2.6 Algorithm Summary

-- CASA Forward Pass at each time step t --

-- Stage 1: Summarize climate state --
COMPUTE Ȳ = mean(Y_{t-1}), σ_Y = std(Y_{t-1})
input_c ← [Ȳ, σ_Y, Niño3.4_t, DMI_t]
c_t ← tanh(W_c · input_c + b_c) -- (d_c,)

-- Stage 2a: Attention scores for all N² province pairs --
FOR each pair (i, j) in {1,...,N}²:
  input_ij ← [Y_i, Y_j, |Y_i - Y_j|, c_t]
  e_ij ← v_a · LeakyReLU(W_a · input_ij + b_a, γ=0.2)
ã_ij ← softmax_row(e_ij) -- row-wise softmax
A_learned_t ← matrix of {ã_ij} -- (N × N)

-- Stage 2b: Blending gate --
α_t ← sigmoid(w_α · c_t + b_α) -- scalar ∈ (0,1)

-- Stage 3: Combine --
W_t ← α_t · A_geo + (1 - α_t) · A_learned_t
F* ← W_t · Y_{t-1}* -- adaptive spatial lag

-- Pass to LSTM (as before) --
x_t ← [Y*_{t-1}, F*, X*_t]
ŷ_t ← LSTM(x_t, h_{t-1}, C_{t-1})

Mathematical Properties

We establish four results. Proofs A and B are foundational (confirm the mechanism is well-defined). Proofs C and D give formal guarantees about model behavior.

Proof A — Why GAT Produces Static Attention

Understanding this result motivates why CASA uses the GATv2 ordering.

Claim (Brody et al., 2022)
In the original GAT, for any two query nodes \(i\) and \(i'\), and any two key nodes \(j_1\) and \(j_2\): if \(e_{ij_1} > e_{ij_2}\), then \(e_{i'j_1} > e_{i'j_2}\). That is, the ranking of any pair of nodes never changes regardless of which query node we consider — attention is monotone, hence static.
Proof A — GAT attention is monotone (static)

Setup. GAT computes attention scores as:

\[ e_{ij}^{\text{GAT}} = \mathbf{a}^\top \text{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i \,\|\, \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j) \]

Partition \(\mathbf{a} = [\mathbf{a}_L \| \mathbf{a}_R]\) where \(\mathbf{a}_L, \mathbf{a}_R\) correspond to the left and right halves of the concatenation.

(1) Since \(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i\) and \(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j\) are independent inputs, and LeakyReLU is applied element-wise after concatenation, the output decomposes:

\[ \text{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i \,\|\, \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j) = [\text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i) \,\|\, \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j)] \]

(2) Therefore:

\[ e_{ij}^{\text{GAT}} = \underbrace{\mathbf{a}_L^\top \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i)}_{g(i)} + \underbrace{\mathbf{a}_R^\top \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j)}_{h(j)} \]

(3) Now compare two key nodes \(j_1\) vs \(j_2\) for query node \(i\):

\[ e_{ij_1} > e_{ij_2} \iff g(i) + h(j_1) > g(i) + h(j_2) \iff h(j_1) > h(j_2) \]

(4) The condition \(h(j_1) > h(j_2)\) does not involve \(i\) at all. Therefore, for any other query \(i'\): \(e_{i'j_1} > e_{i'j_2}\) by the same argument. \(\square\)

Why GATv2 fixes this. GATv2 applies the weight matrix before the nonlinearity:

\[ e_{ij}^{\text{GATv2}} = \mathbf{v}^\top \text{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{W}[\mathbf{h}_i \| \mathbf{h}_j]) \]

Here \(\mathbf{W}\) sees both \(\mathbf{h}_i\) and \(\mathbf{h}_j\) together before the nonlinearity, creating cross-terms that prevent the additive decomposition. The ranking of \(j_1\) vs \(j_2\) can now change depending on \(i\). ✓

Proof B — \(\mathbf{W}_t\) is Row-Stochastic

This guarantees that \(\mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_t \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^*\) is a valid weighted average.

Proposition 1
\(\mathbf{W}_t\) is row-stochastic: \(\sum_j W_t(i,j) = 1\) for all \(i\), and \(W_t(i,j) \geq 0\) for all \(i,j\).
Proof B — Row-stochasticity of W_t

We verify two properties: non-negativity and row sums equal to 1.

(1) \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\) is row-stochastic by construction: each entry is a normalized inverse distance weight, hence non-negative, and rows sum to 1.
(2) \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t\) is row-stochastic because each row is computed via softmax over all \(N\) entries. Softmax always produces non-negative outputs summing to 1.
(3) Row sums of \(\mathbf{W}_t\):

\[ \sum_j W_t(i,j) = \alpha_t \underbrace{\sum_j A_{\text{geo}}(i,j)}_{=\,1} + (1-\alpha_t)\underbrace{\sum_j \tilde{\alpha}_{ij}^t}_{=\,1} = \alpha_t + (1-\alpha_t) = 1 \]

(4) Non-negativity of \(\mathbf{W}_t\): Since \(\alpha_t \in (0,1)\), both coefficients \(\alpha_t > 0\) and \((1-\alpha_t) > 0\). Since all entries of \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\) and \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t\) are non-negative, all entries of \(\mathbf{W}_t\) are non-negative. \(\square\)

Interpretation. \(\mathbf{F}_{t-1}^* = \mathbf{W}_t \cdot \mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^*\) is a convex combination of the normalized rainfall values from all provinces — a weighted average that sums to a meaningful climatological quantity.

Proof C — CASA Subsumes the Static Model

Proposition 2
If the optimizer sets \(b_\alpha \to +\infty\), then \(\alpha_t \to 1\) for all \(t\), and CASA reduces exactly to LSTM-GSTARX with \(\mathbf{W}_t = \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\). The static model is a special case of CASA.
Proof C — Subsumption of the static baseline
(1) Recall \(\alpha_t = \sigma(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t + b_\alpha)\) where \(\sigma\) is the sigmoid function.
(2) The context vector \(\mathbf{c}_t = \tanh(\cdot) \in (-1, 1)^{d_c}\) is bounded. Therefore \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t\) is bounded for any fixed \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha\).
(3) As \(b_\alpha \to +\infty\): for any bounded \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t\), the argument \(\mathbf{w}_\alpha^\top \mathbf{c}_t + b_\alpha \to +\infty\). Since \(\sigma(x) \to 1\) as \(x \to +\infty\), we get \(\alpha_t \to 1\) for all \(t\).
(4) Then \(\mathbf{W}_t = 1 \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} + 0 \cdot \mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t = \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\) — the static geographic weight matrix. \(\square\)

Practical guarantee. Because the static model lies within CASA's search space, gradient descent on training loss will never produce a CASA model that performs worse than the static baseline on training data. If no adaptive pattern is found, the optimizer simply drives \(b_\alpha\) large.

Proof D — Bounded Deviation from Geographic Prior

Proposition 3
For any time step \(t\), the deviation of \(\mathbf{W}_t\) from the geographic prior is bounded: \[ \|\mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \leq (1-\alpha_t)\|\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \]
Proof D — Geographic prior as implicit regularizer
(1) Compute the difference \(\mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\):

\[ \mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} = \alpha_t \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} + (1-\alpha_t)\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} = (1-\alpha_t)(\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}) \]

(2) Taking the Frobenius norm and using scalar factoring (\(1-\alpha_t > 0\)):

\[ \|\mathbf{W}_t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F = (1-\alpha_t)\|\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \leq (1-\alpha_t)\|\mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t - \mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}}\|_F \; \square \]

Interpretation with numbers. In the worked example (Chapter 4), \(\alpha_t \approx 0.32\). So the deviation of \(\mathbf{W}_t\) from geography is at most \(68\%\) of the maximum possible deviation. During normal months (expected \(\alpha_t \approx 0.8\)), this shrinks to at most \(20\%\). The blending gate automatically regulates how far the model can stray from geographic structure.

Practical implication. This acts as an implicit regularizer — no explicit L2 penalty is needed to prevent the learned attention from becoming geographically arbitrary.

4.3 Interpreting the Results

Key Finding: West Papua

The static model sets \(A_{\text{geo}}[3,3] = 0\), so West Papua's spatial lag depends only on P1 and P2 — both drier than average. Result: \(F^*_{\text{static}} = 0.240\).

CASA learns that under El Niño, Papua's own past rainfall is a meaningful predictor for itself (self-loop weight ≈ 0.22) — capturing the phenomenon where eastern Indonesia's anomalously high rainfall is partially self-sustaining during El Niño events. Result: \(F^* = 0.344\), a +43% increase over the static estimate.

This is consistent with observed ENSO teleconnection patterns: eastern Indonesia (Papua) responds differently to El Niño than western regions, and the coherence between these distant provinces changes sign during strong ENSO events (Feng et al., 2010; Kurniadi et al., 2021).

Appendix A

Gradient Derivation (Backward Pass)

All parameters in CASA (\(\mathbf{W}_c, \mathbf{b}_c, \mathbf{W}_a, \mathbf{b}_a, \mathbf{v}_a, \mathbf{w}_\alpha, b_\alpha\)) are learned end-to-end via backpropagation through the LSTM loss. The chain of gradients flows as follows.

A.1 Gradient Through Spatial Lag

From the LSTM backward pass, we obtain \(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{F}_{t-1}^*}\) (elements 39–76 of the input gradient). Then:

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{W}_t} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{F}_{t-1}^*} \cdot (\mathbf{Y}_{t-1}^*)^\top\]

A.2 Gradient Through Blending Gate

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \alpha_t} = \text{tr}\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{W}_t}\right)^\top \!\cdot\! (\mathbf{A}_{\text{geo}} - \mathbf{A}_{\text{learned}}^t)\right]\]

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}_\alpha} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \alpha_t} \cdot \alpha_t(1-\alpha_t) \cdot \mathbf{c}_t \qquad \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b_\alpha} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \alpha_t} \cdot \alpha_t(1-\alpha_t)\]

A.3 Gradient Through Attention (via Softmax Jacobian)

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial e_{ij}^t} = (1-\alpha_t) \cdot \sum_k \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_t(i,k)} \cdot \tilde{\alpha}_{ij}^t(\delta_{jk} - \tilde{\alpha}_{ik}^t) \cdot Y_{t-1,k}^*\]

This gradient then flows back through LeakyReLU and the attention MLP to update \(\mathbf{W}_a, \mathbf{b}_a, \mathbf{v}_a\).

A.4 Gradient Through Context Vector

The context vector receives gradients from two paths:

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{c}_t} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \alpha_t} \cdot \alpha_t(1-\alpha_t) \cdot \mathbf{w}_\alpha}_{\text{path 1: via blending gate}} + \underbrace{\sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial e_{ij}^t} \cdot \frac{\partial e_{ij}^t}{\partial \mathbf{c}_t}}_{\text{path 2: via attention scores}}\]

This gradient propagates through \(\tanh\) to update \(\mathbf{W}_c, \mathbf{b}_c\).
